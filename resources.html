What is moral stress in veterinary medicine?

Moral stress (also called moral distress or moral injury) occurs when a practitioner knows the ethically correct course of action but is prevented from taking it. In veterinary medicine, this frequently involves financial constraints, owner decisions that conflict with clinical judgment, and high-volume euthanasia in shelter settings.

For: Veterinarians, vet techs, shelter staff, and others in animal care roles

Secondary trauma

Recognizing secondary traumatic stress in your team

Secondary traumatic stress (STS) develops through indirect exposure to the suffering of others. In animal care settings, this includes sustained contact with grieving clients, repeated exposure to animal suffering, and cumulative proximity to euthanasia. This resource outlines the specific indicators that distinguish STS from general work stress.

For: Practice managers, shelter directors, team leads

Euthanasia decision fatigue: a clinical perspective

Repeated euthanasia decisions create a specific form of decision fatigue compounded by moral weight. This is distinct from standard clinical decision fatigue. For veterinarians in high-volume practices or shelters performing frequent euthanasia, this accumulates over time and requires specific organizational and clinical responses.

For: Veterinarians, shelter staff, and others making euthanasia decisions

Animal loss

The clinical basis of animal loss grief

Animal loss grief is clinically distinct from human loss grief and is frequently disenfranchised: the individual's experience is minimized by social norms that assign animals a lesser status in the grief hierarchy. Understanding this distinction is foundational to providing effective support, both as a clinician and as a veterinary professional interacting with clients.

For: Individuals, veterinarians, mental health providers

Building a grief-informed veterinary practice

A grief-informed practice is one that acknowledges the emotional realities of veterinary work at the organizational level, provides structured support for staff, and integrates grief-aware communication into client interactions. This resource outlines what a grief-informed approach looks like in practice management terms.

For: Practice owners, clinic managers

Euthanasia

Communicating around euthanasia: what clients need

Clients facing euthanasia decisions need specific kinds of support from the veterinary team: clear information, a validated space for their emotional response, and structured guidance on the process. This resource outlines what effective communication looks like at each stage of the euthanasia conversation, from the initial discussion through aftercare.

For: Anyone in a client-facing animal care role

Navigating animal loss: a guide for individuals

Animal loss is a legitimate grief event. The process of navigating it varies depending on the circumstances: sudden death, euthanasia, terminal illness, or relinquishment each carry distinct dimensions. This resource provides an orientation to the process and outlines when professional consultation is appropriate.

For: Individuals navigating animal loss
