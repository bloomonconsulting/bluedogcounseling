What is moral stress in veterinary medicine?

Moral stress (also called moral distress or moral injury) occurs when a practitioner knows the ethically correct course of action but is prevented from taking it. In veterinary medicine, this frequently involves financial constraints, animal family member decisions that conflict with clinical judgment, and high-volume euthanasia in shelter settings.

For: Veterinarians, vet techs, shelter staff, and others in animal care roles

Secondary trauma

Recognizing secondary traumatic stress in your team

Secondary traumatic stress (STS) develops through indirect exposure to the suffering of others. In animal care settings, this includes sustained contact with grieving clients, repeated exposure to animal suffering, and cumulative proximity to euthanasia. This resource outlines the specific indicators that distinguish STS from general work stress.

For: Practice managers, shelter directors, team leads

Euthanasia Moral stress

Euthanasia decision fatigue: a clinical perspective

Repeated euthanasia decisions create a specific form of decision fatigue compounded by moral weight. This is distinct from standard clinical decision fatigue. For veterinarians in high-volume practices or shelters performing frequent euthanasia, this accumulates over time and requires specific organizational and clinical responses.

For: Veterinarians, shelter staff, and others making euthanasia decisions

Pet loss

The clinical basis of pet loss grief

Pet loss grief is clinically distinct from human loss grief and is frequently disenfranchised: the individual's experience is minimized by social norms that assign animals a lesser status in the grief hierarchy. Understanding this distinction is foundational to providing effective support, both as a clinician and as a veterinary professional interacting with clients.

For: Individuals, veterinarians, mental health providers

Supporting clients through pet loss: a guide for mental health providers

Clinical training on the human-animal bond and pet loss grief is rare. This resource provides an orientation for therapists and counselors working with clients who present with pet loss, euthanasia trauma, or veterinary grief, and outlines where standard grief models require adaptation.

For: Therapists, counselors, social workers

Building a grief-informed veterinary practice

A grief-informed practice acknowledges the emotional realities of veterinary work at the organizational level, provides structured support for staff, and integrates grief-aware communication into client interactions. This resource outlines what a grief-informed approach looks like in practice management terms.

For: Practice owners, clinic managers

Euthanasia

Communicating around euthanasia: what clients need

Clients facing euthanasia decisions need specific kinds of support from the veterinary team: clear information, a structured opportunity for their emotional response, and guidance on the process. This resource outlines what effective communication looks like at each stage of the euthanasia conversation, from the initial discussion through aftercare.

For: Anyone in a client-facing animal care role

Pet loss

Navigating pet loss: a guide for individuals

Pet loss is a legitimate grief event. The process of navigating it varies depending on the circumstances: sudden death, euthanasia, terminal illness, or relinquishment each carry distinct dimensions. This resource provides an orientation to the process and outlines when professional consultation is appropriate.

For: Individuals navigating pet loss
